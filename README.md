## PROJECT BACKGROUND
RavenStack is an early stage SaaS startup that provides digital tools to businesses across multiple industries. The company operates on a subscription based model and currently serves ***500 customers*** across different countries, industries, and pricing plans. As with many growing SaaS companies, maintaining stable monthly recurring revenue (MRR) and minimizing customer churn are important to sustaining growth.
This project explores ***where customer churn is occurring, what customer patterns and behaviors are associated with it and how much recurring revenue is being lost as a result.*** To answer this question, the analysis combines multiple datasets covering customer accounts, subscriptions, churn events, feature usage and support interactions. 

**To answer this business problem, the analysis focuses on three key areas:**
1) **Customer churn patterns**: Examines overall churn levels, churn trends over time, and how churn varies across customer segments such as industries, countries, and subscription plans
2) **Customer behavior before churn**: Analyzes behavioral signals that appear before customers leave, including subscription plan changes, billing frequency, auto renewal status, and support ticket activity. 
3) **Revenue impact of churn**: Evaluates the financial impact of customer churn by tracking monthly recurring revenue lost due to cancellations.

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
Overall, the data shows that ***110 customers have churned**, representing a churn rate of roughly ***22%***, showing  significant risk to the company’s recurring revenue. Churn is not evenly distributed across the customer base. The data shows that certain geographic and industry segments account for a larger share of churn, with the ***United States*** contributing the highest number of churned customers, while the ***Drift Tools industry segment*** records the largest concentration of churn among industries. This suggests that market competition, product fit, or industry-specific needs may influence retention outcomes in these segments.
Customer behavior patterns before churn also reveal important signals. ***A large portion of churned customers did not upgrade or downgrade their subscription before leaving***, indicating that many customers exit the platform without attempting to adjust their plans. Beyond customer loss, churn also has a direct financial impact. As customers cancel subscriptions, the company experiences consistent monthly recurring revenue (MRR) losses, with noticeable revenue declines during periods where churn spikes. 


## INSIGHTS DEEP DIVE 
1) **Customer churn patterns:** The monthly churn trend shows steady customer attrition across the ***two year period, with higher spikes toward late 2024***, suggesting that churn is driven by ongoing retention challenges rather than isolated events. Churn is highly concentrated within specific markets and industries. The ***United States accounts for 68 churned customers (about 62%)***, indicating that the company faces stronger retention pressure in this highly competitive market. From an industry perspective, ***DevTools customers represent the largest share of churn (35 customers, 32%), followed by FinTech (23%) and HealthTech (19%)***, showing that businesses in technology heavy sectors may have higher expectations for product performance and feature depth, increasing the likelihood of switching providers.
      Across subscription tiers, churn appears relatively evenly distributed, ***with Pro (39), Basic (37), and Enterprise (34) customers*** all contributing similar shares. Suggesting that churn is less likely driven by pricing alone and more likely linked to broader factors such as product value, customer experience, or competitive alternatives

2) **Customer behavior before churn:** To better understand the drivers behind churn, ***the analysis examined customers’ last observable behavior before leaving the ravenstack**. The results show that the vast majority of churned customers did not modify their subscription before exiting. Out of the 110 churned users, 101 customers ***(about 92%)** made no plan change prior to churn, while only ***6 upgraded and 3 downgraded*** their subscriptions. This pattern suggests that most customers disengage without attempting to adjust their plan, indicating potential dissatisfaction or declining product engagement rather than pricing related issues. Support interactions provide another signal preceding churn. Customers with higher support ticket volumes show noticeably higher churn levels, with ***82*** churned customers having submitted three or more support tickets. This trend suggests that recurring product issues, unresolved concerns, or friction in the user experience may contribute to customers eventually leaving the platform.
    Billing behavior also highlights differences in churn patterns. Annual subscribers account for ***68 churned customers compared to 42 among monthly users***, showing that long term commitments do not necessarily prevent churn and may reflect dissatisfaction accumulating over a longer subscription period. These patterns suggest that churn at RavenStack is often preceded by signs of disengagement and product friction, rather than proactive subscription adjustments. 
  
  3) **Revenue impact of churn:** Customer churn at Ravenstack does not only reduce the customer base but also creates a direct and compounding impact on recurring revenue. Over the two year period, churned customers account for approximately ***$2M*** in lost monthly recurring revenue (MRR), highlighting the financial significance of retention for the business. The monthly revenue loss trend shows noticeable spikes during periods where churn increases, particularly throughout 2024. As more customers cancel subscriptions, the decline in MRR becomes more pronounced, demonstrating how even moderate increases in churn can quickly translate into meaningful revenue erosion for a subscription-based company.

## RECCOMMENDATION
1. **Strengthen the trial to paid conversion experience**
Trial users show higher churn levels, suggesting that many customers fail to see the saas value early on. RavenStack can improve conversion by implementing guided onboarding, product walkthroughs, and milestone based activation prompts that help users experience key features during the trial period. Ensuring customers quickly reach their “aha moment” can significantly increase the likelihood of long term adoption.

2. **Use support activity as an early churn warning system**
The data shows that a large portion of churned customers had three or more support tickets, indicating unresolved friction before cancellation. RavenStack should treat high ticket volume as a churn risk signal, enabling the customer success team to proactively reach out, resolve issues faster, and provide personalized assistance before dissatisfaction escalates.

3. **Focus retention efforts on high risk segments**
Churn is heavily concentrated in specific markets and industries, particularly U.S based customers and DevTools related companies. By prioritizing these segments with targeted customer success programs, industry specific feature improvements, and competitive positioning, RavenStack can address the unique needs of the customers most likely to churn.

4. **Introduce proactive engagement and customer health monitoring**
Since most churned customers leave without changing their subscription plans, it suggests disengagement rather than pricing dissatisfaction. Implementing customer health scores based on usage, support activity, and engagement levels would allow RavenStack to identify declining activity early and trigger retention strategies such as check-ins, feature education, or value demonstrations.
5. **Protect recurring revenue through retention programs**
With approximately $2M in MRR lost due to churn, improving retention can have a direct financial impact. RavenStack can introduce loyalty incentives, contract renewal touchpoints, and customer feedback loops to strengthen long term relationships and reduce revenue volatility caused by cancellations.
